## The World:

The **Second Sons** trilogy takes place in a somewhat isolated corner of the world. We really only hear about three or four extant countries throughout the story. This fits because the story is less about conquest and more about intrigue (more about that later). The two main countries would be the island Kingdom of Dhevyn, and Senet. Before the story began, Senet had invaded and essentially vassalized Dhevyn. The first reason for this would be religious. Among the many power-brokers behind the proverbial Senetian curtain are the **High Priestess** and her **Shadowdancers**. After a devastating eclipse struck the earth, faith in the *Sundancers* (the Shadowdancers' predecessors) shattered. The High Priestess, according to accounts, put an end to the eclipse (through the sacrifice of the Lion of Senet's son), and ended up displacing the Sundancers' influence. The Queen of Senet had opposed this sacrifice and fled with her child to Dhevyn, her homeland, so to carry out the sacrifice it became necessary to invade Dhevyn. The now-displaced King of Dhevyn formed a sort of government-in-exile in the Baenlands, which will be important later on. The world is small-scale, but the plot is reasonably character-centric, so it fits nicely.

## Its Characters:

The **Second Sons**' is very much a character-based story. In particular, the story revolves around **Dirk**, our protagonist, and his (usually concealed) plots. Dirk is the bastard son of Morna Provin; she had him with Johan Thorn, the former King of Dhevyn, but Thorn was forced into exile and she had him raised as the son of Duke Wallin of Elcast, without him knowing about any of this. Dirk starts off a bit geek-ish, so I was a bit put-off when he suddenly transformed into the **Joker** (without the melodrama) at the near-end of the first book. He certainly does become somewhat of a mastermind, and by the third book it even becomes a running joke. Still, his morals are absolute, and I was surprised to count and find he'd really only killed Thorn (and that too under duress). Obviously, if I told him that he'd deny it and read out his laundry list.

If asked to pick a second character to Dirk, most would no doubt choose **Kirsh**, the **Second Son** of Prince Antonov, the Lion of Senet. The Lion of Senet *had* three sons: the oldest is a druggie cripple (court intrigue), his youngest he killed in the aforementioned sacrifice, and Kirsh. Kirsh runs a sort of parallel life to Dirk; both are 'Second Sons', meaning they're second-in-line to their respective inheritances, and is probably where the name of the trilogy came from. Dirk is the rational counterpart to the emotional Kirsh. Much of the story is the story of the tension between these two.

And the tension usually involves **Marqel**. Marqel is a villain, that's really all there is to it. I can appreciate that sort of straight-forward villainry. It's not like she's a cartoon villain; she's a 3D villain. But she's a villain. I was kind of annoyed that, by the end, other than a lust for power, I found no explanations for her actions. Her character would have been more dynamic, I feel, if we got some indication that she had *some* problems with the path she was taking, at least. Dirk, ever the kind one, told her (multiple times) that she was leading herself on a path to ruin, that living for power usually ends up with you having neither power nor satisfaction. He also tried to warn Kirsh of her duplicity, alongside all the other very obvious signs, but not too different from Marqel, he failed to put his emotions behind him. I'm sure most of the readers were tired of Marqel's shenanigans by the end of the story, but the way he went out earned him some respect in my eyes, at least.

There is a whole coterie of other characters. **Belagren**, the High Priestess, only outmatched by Marqel in villainry, the Lion of Senet himself, the Baenlanders, Neris, the various Dhevynians fighting for their independence, including Dacinta and Alenor. Tia and Misha. All are reasonable characters, and ones I empathized with and raged at alike.

## Its Plot:

The plot generally tracks Dirk's attempt at undoing the Shadowdancers and Senetian influence over Dhevyn, and everybody (including Dirk) trying to figure out the next Age of Shadows (the eclipse) will occur. He first finds himself at Antonov's palace, 'seized' from his parents guardianship because they were teaching him heresy (his mother hates the Senetians and Shadowdancers both, as former mistress of the exiled King of Dhevyn). Court intrigue ensues and he shows himself out of the palace, but not before quite an amazing show.

Next he finds himself with the Baenlanders, a group of exiled Dhevynians who fought with Johan Thorn against the Senetian 'conquest' of their homeland. The Baenlanders act like pirates, and have quite a few connections in the Dhevynian islands. He spends a while here helping the Baenlanders in their pirate-cum-mercantile activities before realizing he can't daddle on forever. He leaves the Baenlanders for the ruins of Omaxin, which supposedly house the secret of when the next 'eclipse' will occur.

Instead, after he finds the 'secret', he betrays the Baenlanders for a spot as the right-hand man of the High Priestess of the Shadowdancers. Belagren built her career on predicting the end of the first eclipse--knowing when the next one begins is necessary. This is already a pretty staggering rise in influence, but he still has higher to go. He appoints a puppet High Priestess when the current High Priestess dies and later takes the position of Lord of the Sun, leader of the Sundancers. He has the new high priestess announce the date of the new eclipse. The problem? There is no eclipse! (I saw this coming the moment he told Marqel to announce it).

When the ceremony for the eclipse arrives, the whole lie is exposed. His faith collapsed, Antonov breaks down, and the whole of Senet seems to follow him. Dirk somehow manages to salvage the situation, including his original goals of dissolving the Shadowdancers and an independent Dhevyn. The focus is generally more on Dirk's individual (and bombastic) plots and plans. The long-term story spans quite a bit of time, but it all fits perfectly.

## Its Themes:

The 'theme' had to be the weakest part of the story. First of all, I'm not even sure if the author had a theme. If I had to say, it'd be some sort of commentary on religion or faith, or perhaps the focus was meant to be on the Dhevynian's aspirations of independence. Either way, the themes were really surface-level. And, honestly, I'd unironically find better critiques of religion on r/atheism. In terms of romance (I don't really care much about this sort of stuff), only two romances were really essential to the plot: Kirsh's (one-sided) love for Marqel, and Tia & Misha. Alexin and Alenor's romance was a decent touch, while Dirk and Jacinta's shtick didn't even begin till the last part of the last book.
