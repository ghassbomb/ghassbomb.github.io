The greatest philosopher of the twentieth-century was also perhaps the greatest eccentric of his time. To understand his philosophy, one must understand his life — and vice versa. Generally, Wittgenstein adhered to an austere, semi-religious lifestyle. In his philosophy too, we can see traces of his mysticism ("whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must remain silent"), and of course his austerity is for all to see in the form of the TLP — laid out more like a proof than a book. His life can be laid out into three epochs in itself.

## His Life

Wittgenstein was born to one of the most prominent families in Vienna — the second richest, after the Habsburgs, some say. His father — Karl Wittgenstein — was quite an interesting figure in his own right, but all we need to know about him is his strict, Puritanism and his wealth as a steel magnate. His mother was neglecting, apparently, and he did not quite enjoy time with her regardless. He dreaded family get-togethers during Christmas and such, and only started enjoying them after *she* died.

The child Wittgenstein (distinct from both the Early, Middle, and Late Wittgensteins) was a complete contrast to popular view of the man: he was an agreeable fellow, and not particularly talented in any manner. The Wittgenstein's of Ludwig's generation were practically all geniuses. Two of his brothers, Hans and Rudolf, the oldest of the bunch, were artistic geniuses. Karl, however, was an industrious man and intended to set them as heirs to his business — with disastrous consequences. Both fled Vienna, one to New York and the other Berlin, and both likely ended up killing themselves.

Karl eased up, luckily for Ludiwg's brother Paul who was *also* an musical genius. His sister Helene was even more appreciated in this regard. The intense competition probably prevented Wittgenstein from picking up any instruments himself (he apparently had Absolute Pitch), though he continued to find solace in music throughout his life.

The Early Wittgenstein was obviously disappointed by his childhood self. As he read Otto Weininger's *Sex and Character* and the various works of Schopenhauer, he extracted many ideas. Notably, he developed a individualistic sense of self, bordering on solipsisim, and a religious penchant for honesty and straight-forwardness — completely in contrast with how he was. These would be the first steps in a continual process of self-improvement that he would work on throughout his life. As he explained later to friends: 'Just improve yourself, that is all you can do to improve the world.'

The work of Weininger deserves a note here for its enduring influence on Wittgenstein. The book itself is quite infamous, and Weininger moreso. In it, he expresses a cultural pessimism; the rise of science and business had put a decline to art, music, and philosophy. This is:

> a time when art is content with daubs and seeks its inspiration in the sports of animals; the time of a superficial anarchy, with no feeling for Justice and the State; a time of communistic ethics, of the most foolish of historical views, the materialistic interpretation of history; a time of capitalism and of Marxism; a time when history, life and science are no more than political economy and technical instruction; a time when genius is supposed to be a form of madness; a time with no great artists and no great philosophers; a time without originality and yet with the most foolish craving for originality

More generally, he attributes this to two Platonic Forms: that of the Jew, and that of the Woman. The Woman is in complete contrast to the man: she is sex-obsessed ('her sexual organs possess women'), and completely incapable of forming ideas (limited instead only to 'henids'). Men are made for greater things. Women, for their incapability, differ little from dogs (and he expresses all the ethical and social consequences of such a view). They are fit only to be Mothers or Prostitutes. The Jew is essentially feminine, and thus suffers from the same faults (as compared to the pinnacle of humanity, the Aryan).

Himself a Jew, and a homosexual, these two facts probably played a part in Weininger's suicide (and also his desire for fame, likely). It is unlikely that Wittgenstein cared much for Weininger's pseudo-scientific racism, however. His interest lay in the passages on Man. For Weininger, Man must be the opposite of the Woman: he must choose will, consciousness, and (Platonic) love. When he achieves this, he becomes Genius. The Genius is superior in all aspects, including philosophy, on which he remarks: 'logic and ethics are no more than duty to oneself'. 

But Man is not born Genius, he has to work towards it and can do so only in one way: a love of God, the Absolute. This God, however, is his own Soul. Men should love the ‘God who in my bosom dwells’. This view would likely inspire Wittgenstein's near sollipsism, and his obsession with self-improvement. Weininger's cultural pessimism would similarly inform Wittgenstein's own, though cultural pessimism was part and parcel of the decaying Viennese culture of the time.

But otherwise, Wittgenstein was educated as a mechanic. He moved eventually to Manchester, where he studied aeronautics. During his study of mathematics, he came upon the works of Russell. Evidently interested, but torn over whether he had the Genius necessary for the topic, he visited Frege first, then Russell in Cambridge. Generally, both agreed he had the talent. Over time, Russell became even fonder of Wittgenstein and considered him his successor.

Wittgenstein found his time in Cambridge stifling, even if it *was* better than the decayed culture of Vienna. It was bad enough that he left for Norway some time later, where he stayed in a remote lodge. It was a fruitful time, however, and he made much progress in his correspondence with Russell. Still, his first work was not yet complete when war broke out in Europe. He had been in Austria at the time, planning a trip with his friend Pinsent. He was told he couldn't, and so he enlisted.

Part of the desire to enlist was likely spiritual. Wittgenstein was, as always, obsessed with self-improvement and he figured he could learn a lot through war. As he wrote, 'Now I have the chance to be a decent human being for I’m standing eye to eye with death.'. Wittgenstein's war experience was initially miserable; ironically, it was his 'comrades' rather than the enemy that made it so. Wittgenstein wouldn't stand 'eye to eye with death' later in the war, but he found the idea more appealing than mingling with the 'crude, stupid and malicious' soldiers of his company.

Still, the spiritual development he sought was received in the form of Tolstoy's '*Gospel in Brief*'. He recommended the book to everyone in doubt and in search of spiritual development. A major idea in this time, one that had intrigued Wittgenstein for much his life, was of the conflict between the internal world of man, and the external world. No doubt spurred by his early readings of Schopenhauer and Weininger, Wittgenstein lay great emphasis on separating the external, uncontrollable influence on a man's life — like, say, war — from the internal. What happens to you externally should, and thus would, not matter to you at all; to be a slave to this external world would simply make you miserable constantly. Instead, man should attend entirely to his internal, spiritual world and ensure that he never 'loses himself'. As he says:

> Don’t be dependent on the external world and then you have no fear of what happens in it … It is x times easier to be independent of things than to be independent of people. But one must be capable of that as well.

Indeed, Wittgenstein was close to losing himself multiple times during the war. In the deadlier days, he often found himself in a two-fold struggle. One was accepting the cruelty and barbarism of his fellow soldiers, 'a company of drunkards, a company of vile and stupid people'; he viewed this as a test of his faith, as 'the heart of a true believer understands everything'. Still, it was a struggle.
