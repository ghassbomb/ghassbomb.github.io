Summary

The dialogue begins with Socrates and Euthyphro providing us some background. Socrates is going to court because a ‘young man’ named Meletus has brought against him the charges of impiety and corrupting the youth. Socrates trial is not the focus of this dialgoue, however. (Euthyphro, 2a-3e)

From 4a-e, Euthyphro explains why he is at court. One of his servants killed Euthyphro’s slave in a drunken fit, and Euthyphro’s father had him thrown into a ditch while he got a local priest. The servant died in the ditch, and Euthyphro claims his father is at fault. He’s got the funny idea that, regardless of the suspect being a murderer, and regardless of the defendant being his father, he has to prosecute him.

Socrates questions whether Euthyphro’s justification for this prosecution is ethically founded, or ‘pious’/‘virtuous’. Euthyphro shows confidence that he is justified, and he claims he knows the Gods would agree with him. This piques Socrates’ interest, and he (likely sarcastically) proclaims Euthyphro his tutor, and himself his pupil. He hopes to discover the nature of piety that Euthyphro seems to have deciphered, in so doing. Or, he might have known that Euthyphro wouldn’t be able to answer all along.

Regardless, Socrates subjects him to his rigorous questioning. Euthyphro first quotes a few ‘particulars’:

I say that the pious is to do what I am doing now, to prosecute the wrongdoer, be it about murder or temple robbery or anything else, whether the wrongdoer is your father or your mother or anyone else; e not to prosecute is impious. (5e)

Which Socrates is obviously not interested in. ‘Bear in mind then’, he says, ’that I did not bid you tell me one or two of the many pious actions but that form itself that makes all pious actions pious’. Thus prompted, Euthyphro gives a more general formulation of how to judge an actions’ piety (putting us close to the Form):

Well then, what is dear to the gods is pious, what is not is impious. (6e-7a)

It sounds sound. Socrates takes over the line, and he leads us on a digression. Euthyphro agrees that the pious is the opposite of the impious. He also agrees that the Gods disagree with each other: ‘[some actions] may be pleasing to Zeus but displeasing to Cronus and Uranus, pleasing to Hephaestus but displeasing to Hera, and so with any other gods who differ from each other on this subject’. He has thus fallen into Socrates trap.

After all, the basis of Euthyphro’s morality is the simply what the Gods deem good and bad. If the Gods, themselves, disagree amongst each other, that basis is extremely shaky if not unfounded. Then, what all the Gods agree on is pious, he says. Socrates, maybe feeling a little mean, rebukes Euthyphro:

Consider this: Is the pious being loved by the gods because (1) it is pious, or is it pious because it is being loved by the gods? (2) (10a)

Euthyphro claims that the pious is loved by the Gods because it is pious, so option 1. But that is a useless answer to Socrates, since it doesn’t help him decipher the Form of the Good. It doesn’t give us any idea of piety, it just gives us a characteristic of it (that it is loved by the Gods).

Option 2 leads to a sort of loop. The pious is such because it is loved by the Gods; but then why is it loved by the Gods? Because it is loved by the Gods? That is not a sufficient answer. We could use option 1; it is loved by the Gods because it is pious. But the flaws in that approach have already been pointed out. So, again, it doesn’t help us deciper the Form of the Good.

Understandably frustrated, Euthyphro leaves after throwing some digs at Socrates.

Exegesis

First, let us take the dialogue at face value. Socrates and Euthyphro are essentially debating questions of morality/ethics, or good conduct: how do we determine whether our actions are good? The word ‘piety’ is often used in English translations, instead of good. They are concerned with piety, and the Gods, because the Greek’s viewed dicussions of morality and the Gods as related. So, it also doubles as theology.

Plato pointing out the paradox at the heart of Euthyphro’s assertion that the pious is what the God’s like (what we call divine command theory) may be viewed as an act of atheism. But Plato was not an atheist, and divine command theory was likely not a concept in his time. The dialogue still raises a tough problem for divine command theorists, regardless; but I’d like to de-emphasize the religious aspects of the text in view of Plato’s theism.

Another focus may be a critique of naive morality. Plato points out the flaws in the moral basis of Greek society at large, embodied in the educated and prominent Euthyphro. The people of Greece are living in Plato’s cave: they have morals, but they have absolutely no idea or reason for these morals. They practice them simply because their ancestors did, with no skepticism.

In this specific case, Euthyphro is prosecuting his father based on a completely unfounded, as Socrates demonstrates, reason. Euthyphro only considers whether his action is just when prompted by Socrates. He is living an unexamined life, which is something Socrates and Plato abhorred. All talk of Gods and piety and other grand ideas is irrelevant when Euthyphro has not considered the consistency of his individual motivations.

Generally, the dialogue may be Plato’s critique of any attempt at formulating a rule-based morality (a particularly modern obsession, since Kant at least). Socrates and Euthyphro try to find that one ‘categorical imperative’ that we may base our actions upon, but fail. Plato, Socrates, and Aristotle were all in favor of what we call ‘virtue ethics’ — it shuns hard-and-fast rules, and the obssession with judging actions (applied ethics, they call it); instead, it gives us a set of virtues to live and act by, allowing more ‘flexibility’.
