How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the AI

By: Ghassan Shahzad
#Economics #Artificial Intelligence
1488 words ~7 mins June 14, 2023

Much hub-bub has been made over the ‘new’ specter of AI that is lurking over the corner of every sector of the economy and is apparently going to kill us all. Technological fears are, of course, not new. I doubt most people buy the extermination nonsense, but I do imagine that quite a few are vaguely worried about losing their jobs, or a general loss in employment. These people miss a general point in economics that I will call upon Henry Hazlitt to explain.

The Distant Past

Firstly, fears of automation increasing unemployment have been constant since about the Industrial Revolution, and even before it! Henry credits it as one of the most ‘viable of all economic delusions’, which makes sense given how disputing it would touch upon multiple disciplines—clearly beyond the layman’s capability. An obvious fallacy in the thought, however, is merely looking at immediate consequences. This is something Henry rails against in his book—‘Economics in One Simple Lesson’—quite a bit. ‘The art of economics consists in looking not merely at the immediate but at the longer effects of any act or policy’ as Henry phrases it.

What does this mean regarding this view on automation? Well, for one, it leads us to the conclusion that man since the invention of fire has been in an endless race to make himself useless. From tools, to the Industrial Revolution, we have put much effort into making ourselves unemployable. This is, of course, an untenable view, but is it not the logical conclusion of fears of automation?

Let us take a tour of history. And who else to start with, but Adam Smith, who has this to say in the ‘Wealth of Nations’:

To take an example, [in] the trade of the pin-maker; a workman not educated to this business, nor acquainted with the use of the machinery employed in it (to the invention of which the same division of labour has probably given occasion), could scarce, perhaps, with his utmost industry, make one pin in a day, and certainly could not make twenty. But in the way in which this business is now carried on, [because of the machinery, they] could make among them upwards of forty-eight thousand pins in a day. (source: Marxists.org)

Alas, even in Adam Smith’s time, machinery was kicking 48,000 pin-makers out of their jobs for every one it kept!—is not something you would think. But let us continue the tour before examining the claims in detail. Soon after Adam Smith came the Industrial Revolution, a catastrophe that—if our fearmongers completed their thoughts—was perhaps worse than the World Wars combined! The Luddites presented the most documented opposition to the Revolution in Britain. These workers organized and burned down new technologies by the boatload, for fear of the machinery ‘replacing’ them. Stocking machinery was a particular target, and yet employment in the stocking industry increased 100x by the end of the century! There was similar opposition to Arkwright’s cotton spinning technologies, but only a decade after his technologies, employment in spinning & weaving had increased by, 4400%!

In the late 19th century, Henry quotes David A. Wells:

During the ten years from 1870 to 1880, inclusive, the British mercantile marine increased its movement, in the matter of foreign entries and clearances alone, to the extent of 22,000,000 tons … yet the number of men who were employed in effecting this great movement had decreased in 1880, as compared with 1870, to the extent of about three thousand (2,990 exactly). What did it? The introduction of steamhoisting machines and grain elevators upon the wharves and docks, the employment of steam power, etc…. In 1873 Bessemer steel in England, where its price had not been enhanced by protective duties, commanded $80 per ton; in 1886 it was profitably manufactured and sold in the same country for less than $20 per ton. Within the same time the annual production capacity of a Bessemer converter has been increased fourfold, with no increase but rather a diminution of the involved labor. The power capacity already being exerted by the steam engines of the world in existence and working in the year 1887 has been estimated by the Bureau of Statistics at Berlin as equivalent to that of 200,000,000 horses, representing approximately 1,000,000,000 men; or at least three times the working population of the earth….

Obviously, this would mean that most men on earth were unemployed, but on our tour do we continue, ignoring these obvious facts.

During the Great Depression, the blame game had targeted automation, spearheaded by a group called The Technocrats. Most of this involved public works, and led to much of the inane insanity associated with government jobs.

Testifying on behalf of the United States Department of Justice before the Temporary National Economic Committee (better known as the TNEC) in March 1941, Corwin Edwards cited innumerable examples of such practices. The electrical union in New York City was charged with refusal to install electrical equipment made outside of New York State unless the equipment was disassembled and reassembled at the job site. In Houston, Texas, master plumbers and the plumbing union agreed that piping prefabricated for installation would be installed by the union only if the thread were cut off one end of the pipe and new thread were cut at the job site. Various locals of the painters’ union imposed restrictions on the use of spray guns, restrictions in many cases designed merely to make work by requiring the slower process of applying paint with a brush. A local of the teamsters’ union required that every truck entering the New York metropolitan area have a local driver in addition to the driver already employed. In various cities the electrical union required that if any temporary light or power was to be used on a construction job there must be a full-time maintenance electrician, who should not be permitted to do any electrical construction work. This rule, according to Mr. Edwards, “often involves the hiring of a man who spends his day reading or playing solitaire and does nothing except throw a switch at the beginning and end of the day.”

An obvious lesson we can extract is that, once machinery has ‘stolen jobs’, taking them back is impossible (unless you don’t mind the insanity outlined in the above paragraph).

Even in the 70s, around the time Henry published (my edition of) his book, there were still fears about automation. Martin Luther King Jr. brought up his fears of the effects of automation on employment in many speeches.

The Problem in the Argument

The argument these technophobes make is that automation and machinery decrease the demand for labor and displace it. But, if they were to stretch this argument out, we could say that solving unemployment would merely involve making all labor as inefficient and unproductive as possible. And yet, inefficient and unproductive labor is a general drain on the economy and is one reason that underdeveloped economies are such. Nobody argues for an inefficient and unproductive populace because that wouldn’t be much better than an unemployed populace. And yet, that is exactly what fearmongers argue for.

But this ignores the fact that their fears, as shown by the historical data above, are simply unfounded. Henry gives us an example:

Let us suppose that a textile manufacturer discovers a technology that reduces labor costs for each of his products by half. He installs said machine and immediately fires half his employees. This seems a cut-and-dry case of lost employment, but let’s take a look at the long-view:

  1. The machine itself had to be made by people, and the manufacturer’s purchase(s) of the machinery may have increased employment in the machine’s production, so that offsets the costs.

  2. In the long-run, the exercise saves the manufacturer money. He ‘accumulates capital’, with which he can either:

    1. He will expand his operations
    2. He will invest it elsewhere
    3. He will spend it on personal consumption

    Either way, he will generate employment, further offsetting the cost.

  3. If he out-competes his competitors, they will either adopt the technology themselves, or they will be absorbed by him. Not only will this generate employment in the industry that manufactures the machinery, it will also drive down costs. Savings will be passed to the consumers, who can then spend it elsewhere, just as the manufacturer could in point 2.

Henry cautions us to not take the argument too far. It is true that economists focus excessively on the short-term to the detriment of the long-term, but that does not mean to ignore the short-term in change. People were in fact thrown out of work by machinery during the Industrial Revolution. But in the long-term, all dues were balanced. Arguments, like ‘it’s different this time!’, weren’t uncommon back then either. I imagine, in the short term at least, all this ‘hype’ will become water in the sand…
